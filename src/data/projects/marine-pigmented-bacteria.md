**Discovery, Functional Characterization, and Biotechnological Potential of Chromogenic Marine Bacteria Associated with *Padina* sp. Collected from Hikkaduwa, Sri Lanka.**

*MPhil Research Project · Department of Botany, University of Sri Jayewardenepura · 2024 – Present*

## Overview

Marine microorganisms represent an emerging reservoir of structurally diverse bioactive
compounds with significant potential for sustainable biotechnological innovation. This
research investigates pigmented bacteria associated with the brown macroalga *Padina* sp.
collected from the Hikkaduwa reef ecosystem of Sri Lanka, with a focus on discovering
naturally derived pigments possessing antioxidant and UV-protective properties. The project
establishes a comprehensive research pipeline spanning microbial prospecting, natural
product discovery, and preliminary industrial evaluation of marine bacterial pigments for
cosmetic and pharmaceutical applications.

## Background

Increasing environmental and health concerns associated with synthetic pigments and
chemical UV-filtering agents have accelerated the search for naturally derived,
multifunctional alternatives. Marine bacteria inhabit dynamic environments characterised by
intense ultraviolet radiation, oxidative stress, and fluctuating physicochemical
conditions, driving the evolution of unique protective metabolites with diverse biological
activities.

Despite Sri Lanka's exceptional marine biodiversity, pigment-producing bacteria associated
with marine macroalgae remain largely unexplored, limiting the discovery of novel microbial
resources for biotechnology. Furthermore, the antioxidant and photoprotective properties of
marine bacterial pigments remain insufficiently characterised, restricting their
translation into sustainable industrial applications.

This research addresses these knowledge gaps through the isolation and characterisation of
pigment-producing marine bacteria from *Padina* sp., evaluating their functional
bioactivities while establishing a scientific foundation for future development of
marine-derived natural pigments as environmentally sustainable bioactive ingredients.

## Methods

Chromogenic bacterial isolates associated with *Padina* sp. collected from the Hikkaduwa
reef ecosystem were systematically isolated and screened to identify high-performing
pigment-producing microorganisms. Comparative microbiological and molecular analyses were
performed to identify representative isolates.

Pigment biosynthesis was optimised through controlled microbial cultivation, followed by
solvent-based extraction of intracellular pigments. Functional characterisation was
conducted using standardised antioxidant assays and spectrophotometric evaluation of
UV-protective capacity to assess the bioactive potential of the extracted pigments. To
evaluate their preliminary biosafety for potential biomedical and cosmetic applications,
zebrafish (*Danio rerio*) embryos were employed as an *in vivo* developmental toxicity model
to assess pigment-induced embryotoxicity.

To facilitate future industrial translation, a laboratory-scale bioreactor system was
designed and developed to optimise cultivation conditions and establish a scalable platform
for the mass production of chromogenic marine bacteria. Complementary microbiological,
biochemical, analytical, and bioprocess engineering approaches were integrated to establish
a reproducible workflow spanning microbial discovery, functional characterisation,
biosafety evaluation, and process scale-up for marine bacterial pigment production.

## Impact & Scientific Contribution

This research investigated several chromogenic marine bacterial species associated with the
brown seaweed *Padina* sp., including *Micrococcus* sp., *Brevibacterium* sp., and
*Chromohalobacter* sp., to evaluate their potential as sustainable sources of high-value
natural pigments. Functional characterisation demonstrated that these bacterial pigments
possess significant antioxidant and UV-protective activities, highlighting their promise as
naturally derived bioactive ingredients for next-generation cosmeceutical formulations.
Their translational potential was further supported through a preliminary biosafety
assessment using the zebrafish (*Danio rerio*) embryo model, which provided initial evidence
of their suitability for cosmetic and biomedical applications.

![Fieldwork on the southern coast of Sri Lanka](/images/projects/marine-pigmented-bacteria/fieldwork.jpg)

To address the challenges of scalable production, a laboratory-scale bioreactor system was
designed and developed to optimise cultivation conditions and facilitate the sustainable
mass production of chromogenic marine bacteria. Collectively, this work expands the
scientific understanding of Sri Lanka's marine microbial biodiversity as a valuable
reservoir of bioactive pigments with industrial relevance. In addition, the project
contributed to the advancement of marine biotechnology through the scholarly review *From
Ocean to Industry: Novel Bio-Innovations with Marine Bacterial Pigments*, which critically
synthesises current knowledge and explores emerging industrial applications and
commercialisation opportunities for marine microbial pigments.
