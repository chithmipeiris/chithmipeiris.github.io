**Development of an Optimized CTAB-Based DNA Extraction Protocol for High-Quality Genomic DNA Isolation from *Piper nigrum* L.**

*Undergraduate Group Research Project · Department of Plant Sciences, University of Colombo · May 2022 – November 2022*

## Overview

High-quality genomic DNA is fundamental to modern plant molecular biology, yet DNA isolation
from *Piper nigrum* remains technically challenging due to the abundance of polyphenols,
polysaccharides, and other secondary metabolites that interfere with DNA purification and
downstream molecular analyses. This project systematically optimised a CTAB-based DNA
extraction workflow by evaluating multiple extraction protocols, lysis buffer formulations,
and tissue disruption methods to establish a reliable, cost-effective alternative to
commercial DNA extraction kits. The optimised protocol provides an efficient foundation for
population genetics, molecular breeding, and functional genomics research in black pepper.

## Background

Black pepper is one of Sri Lanka's major export spice crops and is second only to cinnamon
in terms of spice export value. Sri Lankan pepper is particularly valued because it contains
relatively high levels of piperine, the alkaloid responsible for its pungency. It has become
an increasingly valuable model for studies on genetic diversity, cultivar improvement, and
piperine biosynthesis. However, successful molecular analyses depend on the isolation of
intact, contaminant-free genomic DNA, which is particularly difficult in black pepper
because leaf tissues contain high concentrations of oxidised polyphenols and other secondary
metabolites that co-precipitate with DNA and inhibit enzymatic reactions such as PCR.

Many existing extraction protocols require prolonged processing, multiple purification
steps, or expensive commercial kits, limiting their routine application in research
laboratories. This project addressed these limitations by systematically investigating how
sample disruption techniques and CTAB lysis buffer composition influence DNA yield and
quality, with the objective of developing a robust, reproducible, and economically
accessible extraction protocol suitable for downstream molecular applications.

## Methods

Six genomic DNA extraction protocols were comparatively evaluated using fresh black pepper
leaf tissue to investigate the combined effects of mechanical tissue disruption and lysis
buffer composition on DNA quality and yield. Different grinding approaches, including liquid
nitrogen and acid-washed sand, were assessed alongside modified CTAB extraction buffers
containing various combinations of sodium chloride (NaCl), lithium chloride (LiCl),
polyvinylpyrrolidone (PVP), and β-mercaptoethanol. DNA concentration and purity were
quantified following extraction, while functional DNA quality was validated through PCR
amplification of the Internal Transcribed Spacer (ITS) region and agarose gel
electrophoresis. The optimised protocol was identified by integrating quantitative DNA yield
with successful downstream amplification efficiency, ensuring both high recovery and
molecular compatibility.

## Results

The study successfully identified an optimised CTAB-based extraction protocol capable of
consistently producing high-quality genomic DNA suitable for downstream molecular analyses.
Comparative evaluation demonstrated that the simultaneous incorporation of NaCl, LiCl, and
PVP within the CTAB lysis buffer significantly enhanced DNA recovery compared with
alternative buffer formulations.

Although tissue disruption using acid-washed sand generated higher apparent DNA
concentrations, these extracts consistently failed PCR amplification, indicating that DNA
quantity alone was not an adequate indicator of extraction success. Conversely, liquid
nitrogen-mediated tissue disruption combined with the optimised CTAB buffer produced intact,
PCR-amplifiable genomic DNA while eliminating the need for expensive commercial extraction
kits. The study further demonstrated that the inclusion of β-mercaptoethanol did not
significantly improve DNA yield, allowing unnecessary reagents to be excluded from the
optimised protocol.

Collectively, the research established a reproducible, cost-effective DNA extraction
methodology that overcomes the challenges associated with high secondary metabolite content
in *Piper nigrum* leaves and provides a reliable platform for future molecular genetics and
genomics research.
