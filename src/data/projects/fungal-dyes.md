**Discovery and Process Optimization of Austrocortirubin: The First Report of a Lagoon Lichen-Associated *Talaromyces verruculosus* Producing a Rare Anthraquinone Pigment in Sri Lanka.**

*B.Sc. (Hons) Research Project · Department of Plant Sciences, University of Colombo · 2023 – 2024*

## Overview

The increasing demand for sustainable alternatives to synthetic dyes has accelerated the
exploration of microbial pigments as environmentally responsible bio-colourants. This
project led to the first discovery and characterisation of the rare anthraquinone pigment
austrocortirubin from a lagoon lichen-associated *Talaromyces verruculosus* isolated in Sri
Lanka, while establishing an optimised extraction strategy for efficient pigment recovery.
By integrating fungal biotechnology, fermentation engineering, analytical chemistry, and
industrial application studies, the research developed a complete laboratory pipeline
spanning microbial discovery, process optimisation, chemical characterisation, and
proof-of-concept validation for sustainable pigment production.

## Background

Synthetic dyes remain indispensable to modern industries despite their well-documented
environmental persistence, toxic effluents, and ecological impacts. Microbial pigments have
emerged as promising alternatives owing to their renewable production, structural diversity,
and broad spectrum of bioactivities. Among these, fungal anthraquinones represent an
important class of aromatic polyketides with significant pharmaceutical and industrial
value.

Although numerous fungal pigments have been reported worldwide, lagoon lichen-associated
fungi remain largely unexplored in Sri Lanka, representing a valuable yet untapped source of
novel secondary metabolites. Furthermore, extraction methodologies for rare fungal pigments
are often species-specific, requiring systematic optimisation to maximise pigment recovery
while preserving chemical integrity.

This research addressed these knowledge gaps through the discovery of a previously
unreported pigment-producing fungal isolate and the development of an optimised extraction
protocol for the recovery and characterisation of austrocortirubin. The study aimed to
establish a scientific foundation for future commercialisation of fungal bio-colourants
while contributing to Sri Lanka's emerging natural product biotechnology research.

## Methods

Approximately 150 preserved fungal isolates maintained in the Department of Plant Sciences,
University of Colombo, were systematically screened to identify high-performing
pigment-producing strains. A lichen-associated isolate recovered from *Roccella montagnei*
collected in the Kalpitiya Lagoon was selected following comparative evaluation of pigment
intensity, growth characteristics, and production stability. Morphological observations were
integrated with molecular identification to confirm the isolate as *Talaromyces
verruculosus*.

Pigment biosynthesis was subsequently optimised through submerged liquid-state fermentation
by systematically evaluating the influence of culture pH and incubation temperature on
metabolite production. Recognising the limited availability of optimised extraction
procedures for this rare fungal pigment, a solvent-based extraction protocol was developed
and refined to maximise pigment recovery using ethyl acetate. Purification was achieved
through Thin Layer Chromatography (TLC).

Chemical characterisation of the purified pigment employed complementary spectroscopic
techniques, including UV–Visible spectroscopy, Fourier Transform Infrared Spectroscopy
(FTIR), and Proton Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (¹H-NMR), enabling structural confirmation of
the extracted metabolite. Pigment stability under varying thermal and pH conditions was
evaluated to determine its suitability for industrial applications, with statistical
validation performed using ANOVA, Tukey's HSD, and Kruskal–Wallis analyses.

## Results

This study represents the first report of the anthraquinone polyketide austrocortirubin
being isolated from a lagoon lichen-associated *Talaromyces verruculosus* in Sri Lanka,
expanding the known geographical distribution of this rare fungal metabolite and
highlighting lagoon lichens as a previously unexplored reservoir of industrially relevant
secondary metabolites.

A robust extraction methodology was successfully established, providing an optimised
protocol for efficient recovery and purification of austrocortirubin from submerged fungal
cultures. Fermentation optimisation identified **pH 6.5** and an incubation temperature of
**32 °C** as the conditions yielding maximum pigment production, thereby improving process
efficiency for laboratory-scale cultivation.

Comprehensive spectroscopic analyses confirmed the identity of the purified pigment as
austrocortirubin, a bioactive anthraquinone belonging to the aromatic polyketide family.
The pigment exhibited remarkable physicochemical stability, maintaining structural integrity
between 40 °C and 80 °C and across a broad pH range of 2–12, demonstrating strong potential
for industrial processing under variable manufacturing conditions.

Collectively, the project established an integrated discovery-to-characterisation workflow
that combines microbial prospecting, process optimisation, analytical chemistry, and
industrial feasibility assessment for sustainable fungal pigment production.

## Scientific Contributions

- First report of the rare anthraquinone pigment austrocortirubin from a lagoon
  lichen-associated *Talaromyces verruculosus* isolated in Sri Lanka.
- Developed and optimised a species-specific extraction protocol for efficient recovery of
  austrocortirubin from submerged fungal cultures.
- Optimised submerged fermentation parameters to enhance fungal pigment biosynthesis.
- Confirmed pigment identity through integrated spectroscopic characterisation using
  UV–Visible, FTIR, and ¹H-NMR analyses.
- Demonstrated exceptional thermal and pH stability, supporting the pigment's suitability
  for sustainable industrial applications.
- Established a reproducible laboratory workflow that bridges microbial natural product
  discovery with downstream biotechnological development.

This work has also been developed into a book chapter and a preprint article on sustainable
fungal-derived dyes, and presented at the International Forestry and Environment Symposium —
see the [Publications](/publications) page.
